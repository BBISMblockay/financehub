Business guide

How to use SILO the right way

This guide is for people doing real work in SILO: verifying sales, moving money requests, buying product, collecting receivables, and keeping inventory honest. It explains what each area is for, the correct order of operations, and how to avoid the mistakes that break reporting downstream.

Working principles (read once)

SILO is the system of record for the workflows below. Treat every save as something finance, ops, or retail may rely on later.

  • One business event, one record. Do not submit duplicate payment requests for the same invoice. If something changed, add a note or ask AP to reopen—do not create parallel requests unless your SOP says so.
  • Prove it with attachments. For vendor pay and reimbursements, upload the invoice, receipt, or approval email. AP should not chase missing source documents.
  • Pick the right category. Request types drive reporting and queue filters. Mis-typing (for example vendor payment vs customer refund) slows everyone down.
  • Reference numbers matter. Use invoice #, internal PO #, Flex ID, and due date when your process requires them—they tie SILO to external systems.
  • Status fields are communication. In workbenches (payment queue, PO Builder, AR), update status and notes so the next person knows what happened without Slack archaeology.
  • Do not “fix” data outside the tool without recording it. If you adjust something in a spreadsheet or in Shopify, ensure the canonical SILO tables or syncs still reflect reality, or finance will see a mismatch.
Confidentiality: customer lists, receivables balances, and payroll views are sensitive. Use SILO on trusted devices; sign out on shared PCs.

Using department hubs

Most daily tools open from Finance (/finance.html), Ops, Retail, or Marketing sidebars. The selected page loads inside an embedded viewer.

  1. Use the sidebar to switch tools; use Reload after you save data in a form so the embedded page picks up fresh values.
  2. Use Open in new tab when you need printing, multiple monitors, or a less cramped layout.
  3. Use sidebar Search on Finance when you already know the name of the tool.
Who lands where: finance and exec roles usually start in Finance; operations in Ops; retail in Retail; marketing in Marketing. If your landing page feels wrong, ask an admin to adjust your department or default page on your profile.

Sales verification

Primary tool: /pages/sales-verification.html (includes optional year-over-year store comps). Legacy /pages/sales-db.html redirects there.

Business purpose: imported sales rows (from Shopify and/or store spreadsheets) land in SILO. This screen is for trust but verify: confirm totals, spot exceptions, and compare locations before you rely on numbers in commentary or decisions.

Recommended review flow

  1. Choose a preset window (last 30 / 90 days, YTD, or custom). Match the period you are actually closing or questioning—do not leave “all history” on unless you intend a full audit.
  2. Narrow by location when the question is store-specific (one bad tab in a sheet shows up as one location).
  3. Click Apply so KPIs and rollups recalc on the full filtered dataset, not only the visible page of rows.
  4. Use quick filters for exception hunting: refund discrepancies, blank SKUs, negative net. Clear those filters when you return to normal browsing.
  5. Read Location verification first (rolled up by site). If a location looks off, drill into Row review and paginate through line detail.
  6. Use Search (SKU, product, vendor) when someone sends you a specific item to investigate.
  7. When you find a true data issue, fix it at the source (POS / sheet / Shopify / sync owner)—not by asking someone to “ignore” SILO. Note the date and SKU in your team channel so sync owners can confirm the next run.
Good habit: hit Refresh after you know a nightly sync completed, so you are not reviewing stale totals during month-end.

Payment requests (submitting)

Tool: /pages/purchase-request.html — “Baseballism Payment Request”

Business purpose: this is the front door into AP. You are building a packet AP can pay from: vendor, amount, type, references, and proof.

Before you start

  • Sign in so your request is stamped to your user and your email can pre-fill.
  • Have PDFs or images ready (invoice, receipt, signed approval). Multiple files are allowed.

Choosing request type (do not guess)

TypeUse when
Invoice / vendor paymentA supplier sent an invoice for goods or services and you are requesting payment against that invoice.
Inventory depositFinance-directed deposits tied to inventory programs (use per your internal definition).
Inventory balanceSettlements or true-ups related to inventory balances (not the same as a normal vendor invoice).
Employee reimbursementAn employee paid out of pocket for an approved company expense.
Customer refundYou are returning money to a customer per policy—not paying a vendor.

Completing the form correctly

  1. Vendor: start typing; pick a prior vendor when it is the same counterparty to keep naming consistent. If it is genuinely new, type the legal payee name.
  2. Amount due: enter the amount you are requesting now (match the invoice or approved total; do not net terms in the amount field unless that is your documented process).
  3. References: fill invoice #, Flex ID, internal PO #, and due date whenever they exist—AP uses these to avoid duplicate pay and late fees.
  4. Location / department: add the store or department context if it helps routing.
  5. Notes: explain anything non-obvious (partial pay, credit memo pending, rush reason). If nothing is unusual, a short “standard terms” note is fine.
  6. Submit once. If you made a mistake, do not submit five copies; contact AP or use the workbench process your team defines.

After submit, internal fields (assignment, payment detail, completion) are handled in the Payment Requests Workbench—not on this page.

Payment queue (AP review)

Tool: /pages/request-manager.html — “Payment Requests Workbench” / Payment Queue

Business purpose: compact queue for review, assignment, document checks, payment completion, and reporting. This is where submitted requests become paid, rejected, or sent back for info.

Daily AP flow

  1. Refresh data when you start a session or after you know submitters have been active.
  2. Use Working queues (and the status / priority / due filters) to focus “new”, “needs info”, or “due soon” buckets—not the whole company at once.
  3. Open a row into the drawer. Verify attachments, match invoice totals to the request, and confirm the request type aligns with reality.
  4. Move status with discipline:
    • New → In review when someone owns it.
    • Needs info when you are waiting on the requester (be specific in notes).
    • Approved → Processing → Paid only when payment actually left (or your exact SOP says so).
    • Rejected / Cancelled with a clear note so reporting and the submitter understand why.
  5. Use the Activity tab for audit context—what changed, when, and by whom—before you argue about a number.
  6. Use Reporting for turnaround and due-bucket views; export when finance asks for a frozen snapshot.
Assignment: if your team uses “assigned to”, keep ownership current so the same request is not worked twice or not at all.

PO Builder (purchasing)

Tool: /pages/po-builder.html

Business purpose: build purchase orders tied to the product catalog, edit line matrices, bulk-add SKUs, duplicate an order when repeating buys, and output vendor-facing PDFs. Lines autosave—still treat status changes as formal handoffs.

Correct lifecycle usage

POs move through statuses such as Draft → Approved → Sent to Factory → Confirmed → In production → shipping / transit / partial receipts → Received or Closed (or Cancelled). Advance the status when the business fact changed, not when you “hope” it will.

  • Draft: buyers edit freely. Use clear PO names and factory/bucket fields so others can find the order later.
  • Approved: only after internal sign-off per your threshold (dollar limit, margin check, etc.).
  • Sent to factory / Confirmed / In production: align with factory acknowledgements—do not jump ahead or factories and finance will disagree.
  • Shipped / In transit / Partially received: reflect logistics reality; partial receipts matter for inventory timing.
  • Received / Closed: close the loop when product and invoices are reconciled.

Line grid habits

  • Watch the per-line autosave indicator; fix errors immediately so you do not build 40 lines on a bad SKU.
  • Use catalog pick and bulk tools instead of free-typing SKUs when possible—fewer typos, cleaner receipts.
  • Duplicate PO for repeat buys with small deltas; New PO when season, terms, or vendor differ.

Maintain Factories (/pages/factories.html) accurately—PO Builder and reporting depend on clean factory metadata.

Wholesale AR (collecting money)

Tool: /pages/baseballismwholesale.html — “Baseballism Wholesale AR”

Business purpose: wholesale customers, invoices, and follow-ups in one workbench. Customer and invoice data is fed in part by a nightly sync—assume yesterday’s state unless you have refreshed.

How to work the tabs

  • Queue: your daily call list—who needs a touch, which actions are overdue. Work top-down by priority or aging per your playbook.
  • Customers: relationship view—terms, contacts, and rollup context before you call or email.
  • Orders / invoices: validate open balances against real invoices and credits before you promise a payoff date.
  • Activity: log calls and outcomes so the next collector (or finance during audit) sees the story.

Business rules collectors should follow

  • Log every meaningful touch (promise to pay, dispute, bankruptcy notice) in activity—not only payments.
  • When a dispute is valid, move the process in your SOP (credit memo, adjustment)—do not mark collected in SILO without matching cash.
  • Use export for management summaries; note the export time in email so everyone uses the same snapshot.

Inventory & product tags

Tools: /inventory.html (Inventory Manager / workbench), /pages/product-manager.html (Product tags)

Business purpose: inventory is for operational truth (what is on hand, where it is short, what to move). Product tags are for how you classify and merchandise product in data—not the same as a PO line.

Inventory workbench

  • Start from the filter rail: narrow to the slice you are actioning (location, vendor, risk flags) instead of scrolling the whole company.
  • Use tags and planning fields consistently with the merchandising taxonomy your team agreed on—mixed naming breaks downstream dashboards.
  • When you open a draft or adjustment panel, complete it or cancel explicitly; half-saved intent creates phantom buys.
  • If quantities disagree with the sales floor, verify physical count before bulk edits; SILO numbers may feed purchasing.

Product tags (merchandising)

Use Product tags for attributes collections, campaigns, and reporting expect (collections, caps, gender splits, etc.). Edit in one place; do not maintain a shadow spreadsheet of tags.

Planning, calendars, and external reports

Revenue projections (/projections.html): enter assumptions your finance lead defines—do not overwrite someone else’s scenario without coordination; copy rows or use version discipline if the tool allows.

Calendars (marketing, PO, launch): use them as the shared timeline—if a date slips, update SILO so retail and ops do not plan against a ghost date.

Power BI links from the menu are read-only analytics. Treat them as presentation of SILO and other feeds; if BI disagrees with SILO detail screens, trust the detail screen for the transaction line and open a data investigation.

Jotform grids (travel, bars, etc.): submit structured requests there when that is the official intake; do not duplicate the same spend in SILO payment requests unless process says to.

Payroll BI (/pages/payroll.html): use for authorized payroll review only; never export full registers to personal email.
